Start With Licensing and Insurance

Smart home work often touches your electrical system, your network, and sometimes your door hardware and access points. In Florida, low voltage and electrical work are regulated, so the first thing to confirm is that your installer holds the right state license for the work they are doing. Ask for the license number and look it up. A licensed contractor has met training and exam requirements and can pull permits when a job calls for them.

Insurance matters just as much. A real smart home installer carries general liability coverage and, if they have employees, workers' compensation. If someone gets hurt on your property or a wall gets damaged during a run of wire, you do not want that risk landing on your homeowner's policy. Ask for a certificate of insurance and check that it is current.

Ask for References and Recent Work

A good installer will gladly point you to recent customers in your area. Talk to a few. Ask whether the project finished on schedule, whether the final price matched the quote, and whether the system has held up. Photos of past installations help, but a short phone call with a real homeowner tells you more about how the company communicates when something goes wrong.

Insist on a Clear Written Quote

Never work from a number scribbled on the back of a business card. A proper quote spells out the equipment brands and models, the labor, any wiring or network upgrades, permit costs, and the timeline. It should also note what is not included. When the scope is in writing, you can compare two installers fairly and you have something to point to if the bill creeps up later.

Pay attention to how brands are handled. An installer who supports a range of products, from lighting and shades to cameras, thermostats, locks, and whole-home audio, can build a system around your needs instead of pushing whatever earns them the biggest margin. Questions to Ask Before You Hire

Bring a short list to every conversation. The answers tell you a lot about how the company operates.

  • Are you licensed and insured in Florida, and can I see proof?
  • Will you pull any permits the job requires, or is that on me?
  • Which brands and systems do you install, and why those?
  • Is there any required monthly fee or subscription to keep my system running?
  • What does your warranty cover on parts and labor?
  • Who do I call when something stops working, and what are your response times?
  • Can I control and expand the system myself, or am I locked into your service?
  • Can you provide local references from the past year?

Red Flags to Avoid

Some warning signs are easy to spot once you know to look. Be cautious with any smart home installer who pushes a long monthly contract just to use equipment you already paid for. Some services genuinely cost money to run each month, but you should not be locked into a fee simply to turn your own lights on. Other red flags include a refusal to put the quote in writing, vague answers about licensing, demands for large cash deposits up front, and high pressure to sign today for a "limited" discount.

Also watch for installers who set everything up around their personal account or app. If the system is tied to them and not to you, moving on later becomes a headache. You want admin access to your own home.
